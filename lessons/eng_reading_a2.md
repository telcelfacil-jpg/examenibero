# Oxford Test of English: Reading

The Reading paper checks whether you can understand short messages, notices, emails and longer texts. At A2 level, the goal is not to know every word. The goal is to understand the main message, find key details and choose the option that matches the text best.

## What the test usually measures

- understanding the general idea,
- finding specific information,
- identifying the purpose of a text,
- choosing the correct meaning from context,
- noticing synonyms and paraphrases.

## Part 1: short texts

You may read a sign, a message, a short email or an announcement.

Example:

"Store closed on Monday for repairs. Open normally on Tuesday."

Question: What happens on Monday?

- A) Customers can shop as usual.
- B) Workers are fixing the store.
- C) The store opens late.

Correct answer: **B**.

Why? The text says "closed" and "for repairs". That means the store is being fixed.

## Part 2: matching information

In this task you read short descriptions and match them with people, needs or situations. The answer is often hidden through synonyms.

Example:

- A student wants a quiet place to study.
- One option says: "A silent room with desks and free Wi-Fi."

The words are not exactly the same, but "quiet" matches "silent". This is the kind of relation the exam expects you to notice.

## Part 3: longer text

You may read a short article of about 200 to 250 words. Here you need to understand:

- the main idea,
- the writer's opinion,
- one or two important details,
- the meaning of a word in context.

Example snippet:

"Modern technology has changed how we learn. While some people fear it makes us lazy, I believe it gives students faster access to information and new ways to practise at home."

Possible main idea:

Technology is changing education and can be useful for learning.

## Strategy for A2 readers

### 1. Read the title or text type first

Ask yourself: Is it a notice, an email, an article or an advert?

### 2. Find key words

Look for names, places, times, days, prices and repeated words.

### 3. Do not panic with one unknown word

If you understand the sentence around it, keep going. Many correct answers come from context, not from dictionary translation.

### 4. Compare text and options carefully

The exam often changes the wording. The text may say "cheap" and the option may say "not expensive". Same idea, different words.

## Mini practice

Text:

"Please leave your bikes near the main gate. Students cannot park them next to the library."

Question: Where should students leave their bikes?

- A) Next to the library
- B) Near the main gate
- C) Inside the classroom

Correct answer: **B**.

## Useful vocabulary

- **purpose:** why the text was written
- **attitude:** how the writer feels
- **detail:** exact information
- **gist:** general idea
- **notice:** short public message

## Final advice

In Reading, the best student is not the one who translates every word. It is the one who identifies the text type, scans for clues and checks which option matches the meaning most precisely.
